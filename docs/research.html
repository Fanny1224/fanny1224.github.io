My research at Bambi Group at Fudan university is mainly about utilizing the X-ray data to study the evolution of the system containing a black hole, an accretion disk and a corona (see the figure below). In the case of stellar mass black holes, the thermal radiation from the disk produces a spectrum peaking in the soft X-ray band. The electrons of the corona, which refers to some hot plasma distributed near the black hole, are inversed Compton scattered off by the photons from the disk. Some of these Comptonized photons reaches the infinity, while some can illuminate the accretion disk, and thus produce the reflection spectrum. There are many different models describing the spectral components of this system. We try to use these models to fit the X-ray data from observations, and analyze the evolution of these fitting parameters to see if we can have a better understanding of the properties of the system and how it is changing during and outburst.

An illustration of the black hole - disk - corona model

In my first research, we focus on the HXMT observations of the source MAXI J1820, analyzing the temporal evolution of the spectral parameters in the whole outburst with more than 150 observations. We employ two different models to fit the thermal spectrum of the disk: the Newtonian model diskbb and the relativistic model nkbb. These two models provide different pictures of the source in the soft state, including the change of the corona and the inner disk radius of the source. However, if we change some of the fixed parameters, it is possible to find a consistency between two different models, including the measurement of the black hole spin. In my second research, we study how different geometries of the corona will influence the Comptonized thermal spectrum. It's common to use the phenomenological cut-off powerlaw model to describe the scattering effect. However, in this description, we can't have more knowledge of the corona geometry (i.e., how the hot plasma is distributed around the accretion disk). Therefore, we utilize the public code MONK, which uses the Monte Carlo method to simulate the photons radiated by the disk and then scattered by the corona in different corona geometries. Then we use the model simplcutx*kerrbb to fit the simulated spectra from MONK, and see how fitting parameters of simplcutx (e.g., the powerlaw index gamma, the scattering fraction and the temperature of the corona) differs in different corona geometries. By comparing the results with the value of these parameters found in the fittings of observed data, we will be able to see which corona geometries are more possible than other ones.

My research at SHAO is mainly about using machine learning to perform the foreground removal of the simulated 21cm signal from the Epoch of Reionization (EoR) observed by the Square Kilometer Array (SKA). Traditional methods of the foreground removal of the 21cm signal depends on the assumption that the foreground signal are smooth compared with 21cm signal, however, in the case of SKA, the complex beam effects and the point spread function (PSF) make the traditional methods difficult to work. Therefore, we try to use the convolutional neural network to improve the performance. The first part of the network is fork-structured, and the dirty skymap (the 21cm signal with all kinds of noises) and the PSF are put together into it. The output of this network is the clean skymap, with the PSF removed from the dirty skymap. Then the predicted clean skymap is put into the second part of the network, which is expected to extract the 21cm signal from the clean skymap.
